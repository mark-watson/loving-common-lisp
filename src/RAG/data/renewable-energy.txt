Renewable Energy Sources and Technologies

Solar energy is one of the fastest-growing renewable energy sources worldwide. Photovoltaic (PV) cells convert sunlight directly into electricity using semiconductor materials, primarily silicon. The efficiency of commercial solar panels has improved from about 6% in the 1950s to over 22% in modern monocrystalline panels. Concentrated solar power (CSP) systems use mirrors or lenses to focus sunlight onto a receiver, generating heat that drives a steam turbine.

Wind energy harnesses the kinetic energy of moving air through wind turbines. Modern onshore wind turbines typically have a capacity of 2-5 megawatts, while offshore turbines can exceed 15 megawatts. Denmark generates over 50% of its electricity from wind power. The main challenge with wind energy is its intermittent nature — wind doesn't blow consistently, requiring energy storage or backup power sources.

Hydroelectric power is the largest source of renewable electricity globally, accounting for about 16% of world electricity production. Large-scale hydroelectric dams like the Three Gorges Dam in China can generate over 22,000 megawatts. Run-of-river systems and small-scale hydroelectric installations provide power without large reservoirs. Pumped-storage hydroelectricity is currently the most common form of grid-scale energy storage, using excess electricity to pump water uphill and releasing it through turbines during peak demand.

Energy storage is a critical challenge for renewable energy adoption. Lithium-ion batteries dominate the current market for grid-scale storage, but their capacity degrades over time and they require materials like lithium, cobalt, and nickel that have limited supply chains. Alternative storage technologies include flow batteries (using vanadium or zinc-bromine), compressed air energy storage (CAES), and hydrogen production through electrolysis. The cost of lithium-ion battery storage has fallen by approximately 90% since 2010, from over $1,100 per kilowatt-hour to under $140 per kilowatt-hour.

Grid integration of renewable energy requires sophisticated management systems. Smart grids use digital communication technology to detect and react to local changes in usage. Virtual power plants aggregate distributed energy resources like rooftop solar panels and home batteries to function as a single power plant. The intermittency problem is being addressed through geographic diversification, demand response programs, and improved weather forecasting for wind and solar output prediction.
