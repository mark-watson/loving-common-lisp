Climate Science: Understanding Earth's Changing Climate

The greenhouse effect is a natural process where certain gases in Earth's atmosphere trap heat from the sun, keeping the planet warm enough to support life. The primary greenhouse gases are carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O), and fluorinated gases. Without the natural greenhouse effect, Earth's average temperature would be about -18°C (0°F) instead of the current 15°C (59°F).

Human activities have significantly increased atmospheric CO2 concentrations. Pre-industrial levels were approximately 280 parts per million (ppm). As of 2024, atmospheric CO2 has reached over 420 ppm — a 50% increase. The burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil, and natural gas) is the largest source of human-caused CO2 emissions, responsible for approximately 75% of global greenhouse gas emissions. Deforestation and land-use changes contribute another 10-15%.

Global average temperatures have risen by approximately 1.1°C (2.0°F) since the pre-industrial era (1850-1900). The rate of warming has accelerated: the last decade (2014-2023) was the warmest on record. Climate models project that without significant emission reductions, global temperatures could rise by 2.5-4.5°C by 2100 under high-emission scenarios. The Paris Agreement aims to limit warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels.

Sea level rise is one of the most visible consequences of climate change. Global mean sea level has risen by about 20 centimeters (8 inches) since 1900, with the rate of rise accelerating in recent decades. Thermal expansion of ocean water accounts for about one-third of sea level rise, while melting glaciers and ice sheets contribute the remainder. The Greenland ice sheet contains enough ice to raise global sea levels by 7.2 meters (24 feet) if it melted completely. Current projections estimate 0.3-1.0 meters of additional sea level rise by 2100.

Carbon capture and storage (CCS) technologies aim to reduce CO2 emissions from industrial sources. Direct air capture (DAC) systems can remove CO2 directly from the atmosphere. Currently, DAC costs between $250-600 per ton of CO2 removed, but costs are expected to fall as technology improves. Natural carbon sinks — forests, oceans, and soil — currently absorb about 50% of human CO2 emissions. Protecting and expanding these natural sinks through reforestation and improved land management is considered essential for meeting climate targets.

The carbon footprint of transportation is significant, accounting for about 16% of global greenhouse gas emissions. Road vehicles alone produce approximately 11.9% of global emissions. Transitioning to electric vehicles powered by renewable energy is considered one of the most effective strategies for reducing transportation emissions. Aviation contributes about 2.5% of global CO2 emissions, and maritime shipping accounts for approximately 2.9%.
